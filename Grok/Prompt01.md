**Cloud computing** is the delivery of computing services—such as servers, storage, databases, networking, software, analytics, and AI—over the internet ("the cloud") rather than owning and maintaining physical hardware locally.

Instead of buying and managing your own servers or data centers, you rent what you need from large providers and access it on-demand, usually paying only for what you use.

### Core Idea
Think of it like electricity: You don't build your own power plant to use a lamp—you plug into the grid and pay for what you consume. Cloud computing lets you tap into massive shared computing resources managed by companies like Amazon, Microsoft, or Google.

### Key Characteristics (NIST Definition)
- **On-demand self-service**: Provision resources instantly without human intervention.
- **Broad network access**: Available over the internet on various devices.
- **Resource pooling**: Providers pool resources to serve many users (multi-tenancy).
- **Rapid elasticity**: Scale resources up or down quickly as needs change.
- **Measured service**: Usage is monitored, and you pay accordingly (like a utility bill).

### Main Service Models
| Model     | Full Name                  | What You Get                              | Examples                          | Best For |
|-----------|----------------------------|-------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|----------|
| **IaaS**  | Infrastructure as a Service | Virtual machines, storage, networks       | AWS EC2, Google Compute Engine   | Full control, custom setups |
| **PaaS**  | Platform as a Service      | Development platform, databases, tools    | Heroku, Google App Engine, Azure App Service | Developers building apps fast |
| **SaaS**  | Software as a Service      | Ready-to-use applications                 | Gmail, Netflix, Microsoft 365, Salesforce | End users |

### Deployment Models
- **Public Cloud**: Services owned and operated by third parties (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud).
- **Private Cloud**: Dedicated infrastructure for one organization (on-premises or hosted).
- **Hybrid Cloud**: Combination of public + private, allowing data/applications to move between them.
- **Multi-Cloud**: Using multiple public cloud providers.

### Benefits
- **Cost savings**: Shift from capital expenses (buying hardware) to operating expenses.
- **Scalability**: Handle traffic spikes without over-provisioning.
- **Speed & Agility**: Deploy new resources in minutes.
- **Reliability**: Major providers offer high uptime (99.99%+), redundancy, and global data centers.
- **Security & Compliance**: Providers invest heavily in security; many offer compliance certifications.
- **Global reach**: Low-latency access from anywhere.

### Common Use Cases
- Running websites and web apps
- Storing and backing up data
- Big data analytics and machine learning
- Software development and testing
- Streaming services (Netflix runs on AWS)
- Enterprise applications (email, CRM, collaboration tools)

### Popular Providers
- **Amazon Web Services (AWS)** — Market leader
- **Microsoft Azure**
- **Google Cloud Platform (GCP)**
- Others: Oracle Cloud, IBM Cloud, Alibaba Cloud, etc.

**In short**: Cloud computing turns computing from a product you buy and maintain into a service you consume, making technology more accessible, flexible, and cost-effective for individuals, startups, and large enterprises alike.
